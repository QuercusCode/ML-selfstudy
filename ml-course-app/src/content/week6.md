# Week 7: Statistical Inference: MLE, MAP, and the Exponential Family

> **Goal:** Connect probability to *fitting models*. MLE is the secret identity of almost all loss functions. If you understand MLE, you understand why we use MSE for regression and Cross-Entropy for classification.

---

## Part 1: Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Suppose you have a dataset $D = \{x_1, x_2, \dots, x_N\}$ drawn independently from some distribution parameterized by $\theta$.
The **Likelihood** of the data given the parameters is the joint probability of seeing all the data points:

$$ L(\theta) = P(D | \theta) = \prod_{i=1}^N P(x_i | \theta) $$

Since multiplying many small probabilities causes numerical underflow, we almost always maximize the **Log-Likelihood** instead. Logarithms turn products into sums, and since $\log$ is monotonically increasing, maximizing $\log L(\theta)$ is identical to maximizing $L(\theta)$.

$$ \ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log P(x_i | \theta) $$

**Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)** simply asks: *What value of $\theta$ maximizes this log-likelihood?*
We find it by taking the derivative $\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta}$, setting it to 0, and solving for $\theta$.

### 1.1 MLE for a Gaussian Distribution
Imagine $N$ data points drawn from a Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. We want to estimate the true $\mu$.

$$ \ell(\mu, \sigma^2) = \sum_{i=1}^N \log \left( \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \right) $$
$$ \ell(\mu, \sigma^2) = \sum_{i=1}^N \left[ -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right] $$

To find the MLE for $\mu$, take the derivative with respect to $\mu$:
$$ \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu} = \sum_{i=1}^N \frac{2(x_i - \mu)}{2\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} \sum_{i=1}^N (x_i - \mu) = 0 $$
$$ \sum_{i=1}^N x_i - N\mu = 0 \implies \hat{\mu}_{MLE} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N x_i $$

The MLE for the Gaussian mean is simply the **sample mean**!

## Part 2: The Secret Identity of Loss Functions

### 2.1 Why do we use Mean Squared Error (MSE)?
In linear regression, we assume our targets $y_i$ are generated by a deterministic line plus some Gaussian noise:
$$ y_i = \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i + \epsilon_i, \quad \text{where } \epsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) $$
This means $y_i$ is distributed as $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i, \sigma^2)$.

Let's find the MLE for $\mathbf{w}$! We maximize the log-likelihood:
$$ \ell(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \left[ -\frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{(y_i - \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)^2}{2\sigma^2} \right] $$

We want to find the $\mathbf{w}$ that maximizes this. The first term doesn't depend on $\mathbf{w}$. The $1/2\sigma^2$ is just a constant scale factor. To maximize a negative quantity, we must **minimize the positive quantity**:

$$ \text{Minimize } \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)^2 $$

This is exactly the **Sum of Squared Errors (SSE)**!
*Conclusion: Minimizing MSE is mathematically equivalent to Maximum Likelihood Estimation assuming Gaussian noise.*

### 2.2 Why do we use Cross-Entropy for Classification?
In a coin flip (binary classification), the outcome $y_i \in \{0, 1\}$ follows a Bernoulli distribution.
Let $\hat{y}_i$ be the predicted probability that $y_i = 1$. The likelihood of observing $y_i$ is:
$$ P(y_i | \hat{y}_i) = \hat{y}_i^{y_i} (1 - \hat{y}_i)^{(1 - y_i)} $$

The log-likelihood over the dataset is:
$$ \ell(\mathbf{w}) = \sum_{i=1}^N \left[ y_i \log(\hat{y}_i) + (1 - y_i) \log(1 - \hat{y}_i) \right] $$

To maximize this likelihood, we minimize its negative. The negative of this log-likelihood is the **Binary Cross-Entropy Loss**.
*Conclusion: Minimizing Cross-Entropy is mathematically equivalent to Maximum Likelihood Estimation assuming Bernoulli outcomes.*

## Part 3: Maximum a Posteriori (MAP) and Regularization

MLE finds the parameters that best fit the data. But if you have very little data, MLE will overfit horribly. (If you flip a coin once and it lands Heads, MLE says the coin will *always* land heads).

We fix this using Bayes' Theorem. Instead of maximizing $P(D | \theta)$, we maximize the Posterior $P(\theta | D)$.
$$ P(\theta | D) \propto P(D | \theta) P(\theta) $$

Take the log:
$$ \log P(\theta | D) = \log P(D | \theta) + \log P(\theta) $$

This is **Maximum a Posteriori (MAP)** estimation. It is simply the Log-Likelihood PLUS a Prior term.

### 3.1 MAP and L2 Regularization (Weight Decay)
Suppose we put a Gaussian prior on our weights, assuming they are centered around 0 with variance $\lambda^{-1}$.
$P(\mathbf{w}) = \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \lambda^{-1} I) \implies \log P(\mathbf{w}) \propto -\frac{\lambda}{2} \|\mathbf{w}\|^2$

If we do MAP estimation for linear regression (Gaussian likelihood + Gaussian prior), we get:
$$ \text{Minimize } \sum_{i=1}^N (y_i - \mathbf{w}^T \mathbf{x}_i)^2 + \frac{\lambda}{2} \|\mathbf{w}\|^2 $$

This is exactly **Ridge Regression (L2 Regularization)**!
*Conclusion: L2 Regularization is mathematically equivalent to placing a Gaussian Prior on your weights.*

### Exercise 2: Code Verification of MLE
Let's verify that L2 Regularization shrinks weights exactly as MAP theory suggests.

```python
import numpy as np

# Create data
np.random.seed(42)
N = 10
x = np.linspace(0, 10, N)
X = np.vstack([np.ones(N), x]).T
# True weights: bias=2, slope=3. Add some noise.
y = 2 + 3 * x + np.random.randn(N) * 2

# 1. MLE (Standard Linear Regression)
# w = (X^T X)^-1 X^T y
w_mle = np.linalg.solve(X.T @ X, X.T @ y)

# 2. MAP (Ridge Regression / L2 Regularization)
# w = (X^T X + lambda*I)^-1 X^T y
lam = 50.0  # Strong prior pulling weights to 0
I = np.eye(2)
I[0, 0] = 0 # Don't regularize the bias term
w_map = np.linalg.solve(X.T @ X + lam * I, X.T @ y)

print(f"MLE Weights: {w_mle}")
print(f"MAP Weights: {w_map}")
# The MAP slope should be significantly lower than the MLE slope!
```

## Self-Test Questions
1. **Why do we maximize Log-Likelihood instead of Likelihood?** *(Multiplying many probabilities $<1$ causes numerical underflow. Logarithms turn products into sums, which are numerically stable. Also, derivatives of sums are much easier than derivatives of massive products.)*
2. **What assumption about your data's noise leads to the Mean Squared Error loss function?** *(Assuming the noise is normally distributed/Gaussian.)*
3. **If you have an infinite amount of data, what happens to the difference between MLE and MAP estimates?** *(They become identical. The Likelihood term scales with $N$, while the Prior term stays constant. As $N \to \infty$, the data completely overwhelms the prior.)*
